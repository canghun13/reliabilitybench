Reliability practice

Use MTBF as an exposure measure, not a promise

MTBF is observed operating time divided by counted failures. Define the asset population, time in service, and failure rule before comparing shifts, sites, vendors, or designs. A small failure count has high uncertainty, and a changing maintenance strategy can change the apparent result.

Downtime economics

Build downtime cost from contribution, not revenue

Estimate units not produced, contribution margin per unit, direct labor, expedited materials, recovery effort, contractual penalties, and safely avoided losses. Use a common event boundary so the same cost is not recorded twice in lost production and response cost.

Maintenance operations

Keep backlog separate from unplanned demand

Backlog should represent approved, estimated, ready work. It becomes a planning signal only when its labor hours are compared with real available capacity after leave, training, meetings, access limits, and planned work windows.

MRO planning

Use demand models and critical-spare policy together

Reorder-point logic controls repeatable demand through known replenishment lead time. Criticality policy handles rare, high-consequence, long-lead items where historic demand is too sparse to be a safe predictor.

How we frame a practical guide

ReliabilityBench avoids universal target values. A useful target depends on the asset function, operating regime, production constraint, risk tolerance, and the quality of the underlying event data. Use these pages to establish shared definitions first, then set the target with the people who own the work.
